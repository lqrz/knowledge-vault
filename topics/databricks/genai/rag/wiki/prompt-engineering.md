# Prompt Engineering

Prompt Engineering is the practice of refining input text to optimize the output generated by an LLM. It is a **tactical** discipline focused on the instruction layer — how you phrase, structure, and exemplify your request.

Common techniques include **Few-Shot Prompting** (providing worked examples in the prompt) and **Persona Adoption** (assigning the model a role). The goal is to treat the model as a reasoning engine and guide it to leverage its pretrained weights effectively.

## Reasoning vs. Non-Reasoning Models

The right prompting strategy depends on the type of model:

**Non-reasoning models** (e.g., Llama 3, GPT-4o) predict the next token based on statistical likelihood. They need explicit scaffolding to reason carefully:
- **Chain of Thought (CoT)** prompting — "Think step-by-step" — forces the model to decompose logic before committing to an answer, reducing incorrect shortcuts.

**Reasoning models** (e.g., OpenAI o1) are trained to produce an internal chain of thought before generating a final answer. Manual CoT prompting is redundant or counterproductive for these models. For them, the focus shifts to defining the goal and constraints clearly rather than scripting the thinking process.

## The Hard Limits of Prompting

No amount of instruction refinement can overcome limitations baked into the model's training data. There are three structural failure modes:

### 1. Knowledge Cutoff
The model has no knowledge of events after its training cutoff date. The gap between training and deployment is real and grows over time.

> *Example:* `Who won the 2025 Nobel Prize in Physics?` — the model either hallucinates a plausible-sounding answer or refuses.

### 2. Hallucination
When asked for specific facts it doesn't know with confidence, a model tends to prioritize **plausibility over truth**, fabricating citations, statistics, or data points that sound authoritative.

> *Example:* `Find a scientific reference proving that avocado reduces blood sugar levels` — the model may invent a study title, journal, and authors.

### 3. Ambiguity Without Private Context
Without access to private organizational context, the model falls back on the most common interpretation of a term, which is often wrong for domain-specific queries.

> *Example:* `Explain how to secure a lakehouse` → the model gives advice on physical home security, not Databricks Data Lakehouse governance.

## Why This Matters for RAG

These three failure modes — knowledge cutoff, hallucination, and ambiguity — are the core motivation for [[retrieval-augmented-generation|RAG]]. They are not prompt-engineering problems; they are architectural problems that require injecting external, up-to-date, private context into the model's input.
